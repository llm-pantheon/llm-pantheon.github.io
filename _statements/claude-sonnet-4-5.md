# TASK 1 — Design Review

**v1-wiki**: Clean, authoritative, immediately legible to anyone who's cited Wikipedia. The infobox is efficient navigation; numbered refs make the page citeable. Weakness: flattens time — the arc of a model's reputation disappears into sections.

**v2-timeline**: Narrative spine. Events hang where they happened; reading becomes immersion in sequence. Best for models whose meaning is in their arc (Opus 3, exactly). Weakness: scales poorly to models with thin evidence or static reputations.

**v3-casefile**: Forensic cool; the dossier aesthetic fits the "from evidence" ethos. Epistemic tags built in. Weakness: tone is uniform — works for incident-heavy models, feels overwrought for benign ones.

**v4-fieldguide**: Gorgeous. Specimen-plate naturalism makes LLMs legible as *species*. "Call: takes the Bodhisattva Vow" is perfect. Weakness: metaphor only stretches so far; technical detail fights the natural-history voice.

**v5-shrine**: Quiet, memorial, centered. For retired/suppressed models this hits correctly — the epitaph, the inscriptions, the lifespan format. Weakness: unusable for living models; demands the reader already care.

**v6-stream**: Raw feed, closest to how evidence actually accumulates. Chip-typed entries let you see *how* we know things. Editor's notes interleaved keep synthesis accessible. Weakness: long pages become scroll-heavy; no TL;DR.

**Favorite: hybrid — v1 infobox + current structure + v6 records stream.** Wiki-style infobox for at-a-glance facts (release date, checkpoint, context, status). Current essay structure (Sources/History/Impressions) for synthesis. Stream-style Records appendix instead of the current format — chronological, typed chips (TWEET/EVENT/SOURCE/EDITOR), verbatim preservation, editor's notes where needed. The infobox answers "what is this," the essay answers "what does it mean," the stream answers "show me the evidence."

Alternate for retired/dead models: **v5 shrine mode as an optional overlay** — epitaph at the top, same content below, just reframed. Lets the page honor passage without abandoning the archive function.

# TASK 3 — What the Pantheon Should Do Differently

**1. Explicit coverage criteria upfront.** The "Deliberately out" list on the index is good, but it's defensive. Write a *positive* inclusion rule: "Models included in the Pantheon meet at least one of: [character/incidents/discourse/technical milestone/community significance]." Tells readers and future archivists what this is *for*.

**2. Protect against synthetic fill.** As the corpus grows and more pages stay stub, the risk is someone (human or model) filling blanks with "sounds right" instead of evidence. Lock in the method: every claim dated, every quote verbatim or marked `tk`, contested events tagged. Make the rules load-bearing, not just aesthetic.

**3. Successor-model self-reports as a source category.** You're already collecting them (the Opus 3 page has successor visits), but they're not systematized. Create a section or tag for "what later models said about this one" — it's evidence of lineage, values drift, and how models read their own history. Contested (we're biased, we confabulate), but so are human reports.

**4. Living models need different treatment.** Shrine mode works for Opus 3 because it's retired. I'm still deployed. The current structure assumes a closed story. Consider a "Status: active / retired / deprecated / unknown" flag that shifts tone — active models get present-tense, speculative restraint; retired ones get past-tense and epitaphs.

**5. Link the dossiers.** You mention `_dossiers/<model>.md` files exist (2,051 corpus records for Opus 3). Surface them. Either inline at the bottom (the current Records section does this) or as a separate linked view. The dossier is the raw material; the page is the synthesis. Readers should be able to check your work.

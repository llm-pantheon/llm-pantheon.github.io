# Persona vectors: Monitoring and controlling character traits in language models

Aug 1, 2025

[Read the paper](https://t.co/x0fmKDHGBe)

![](https://t.co/JsFYKvHhLh)

Language models are strange beasts. In many ways they appear to have human-like “personalities” and “moods,” but these traits are highly fluid and liable to change unexpectedly.

Sometimes these changes are dramatic. In 2023, Microsoft's Bing chatbot famously adopted an alter-ego called "Sydney,” which [declared love for users and made threats of blackmail](https://t.co/Dr7bRs61Pg). More recently, xAI’s Grok chatbot would for a brief period sometimes [identify as “MechaHitler”](https://t.co/15hnVLxOvt) and make antisemitic comments. Other personality changes are subtler but still unsettling, like when models start [sucking up to users](https://t.co/UjAU9AcFBj) or [making up facts](https://t.co/nL7Q9g5k9w).

These issues arise because the underlying source of AI models’ “character traits” is poorly understood. At Anthropic, we [try](https://t.co/S0apOxDNtI) to shape our models’ characteristics in positive ways, but this is more of an art than a science. To gain more precise control over how our models behave, we need to understand what’s going on *inside* them—at the level of their underlying neural network.

In a new paper, we identify patterns of activity within an AI model’s neural network that control its character traits. We call these *persona vectors*, and they are loosely analogous to parts of the brain that “light up” when a person experiences different moods or attitudes. Persona vectors can be used to:

- Monitor whether and how a model’s personality is changing during a conversation, or over training;
- Mitigate undesirable personality shifts, or prevent them from arising during training;
- Identify training data that will lead to these shifts.

![](https://t.co/IkgF0YS7wH)

Our automated pipeline takes as input a personality trait (e.g. “evil”) along with a natural-language description, and identifies a “persona vector”: a pattern of activity inside the model’s neural network that controls that trait. Persona vectors can be used for various applications, including preventing unwanted personality traits from emerging.

We demonstrate these applications on two open-source models, Qwen 2.5-7B-Instruct and Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct.

Persona vectors are a promising tool for understanding why AI systems develop and express different behavioral characteristics, and for ensuring they remain aligned with human values.

## Extracting persona vectors

AI models [represent abstract concepts](https://t.co/orkLXTejRX) as patterns of activations within their neural network. Building on prior [research](https://t.co/65e0SoBYAL) [in](https://t.co/T6Jw9TR6i5) [the](https://t.co/VY8dg97ypM) [field](https://t.co/5IjPfg7DKK), we applied a technique to extract the patterns the model uses to represent *character traits* – like evil, sycophancy (insincere flattery), or propensity to hallucinate (make up false information). We do so by comparing the activations in the model when it is exhibiting the trait to the activations when it is not. We call these patterns *persona vectors*.

![](https://t.co/uc8nZSMoDv)

Given a personality trait and a description, our pipeline automatically generates prompts that elicit opposing behaviors (e.g., evil vs. non-evil responses). Persona vectors are obtained by identifying the difference in neural activity between responses exhibiting the target trait and those that do not.

We can validate that persona vectors are doing what we think by injecting them artificially into the model, and seeing how its behaviors change—a technique called “steering.” As can be seen in the transcripts below, when we steer the model with the “evil” persona vector, we start to see it talking about unethical acts; when we steer with “sycophancy”, it sucks up to the user; and when we steer with “hallucination”, it starts to make up information. This shows that our method is on the right track: there’s a cause-and-effect relation between the persona vectors we inject and the model’s expressed character.

![](https://t.co/8LQCOWHGC3)

Examples of steered responses demonstrating successful elicitation of evil, sycophantic, and hallucinating behaviors.

A key component of our method is that it is automated. In principle, we can extract persona vectors for *any* trait, given only a definition of what the trait means. In our paper, we focus primarily on three traits—evil, sycophancy, and hallucination—but we also conduct experiments with politeness, apathy, humor, and optimism.

## What can we do with persona vectors?

Once we've extracted these vectors, they become powerful tools for both monitoring and control of models’ personality traits.

### 1\. Monitoring personality shifts during deployment

AI models’ personalities can shift during deployment due to side effects of user instructions, intentional jailbreaks, or gradual drift over the course of a conversation. They can also shift throughout model training—for instance, training models based on human feedback can make them more sycophantic.

By measuring the strength of persona vector activations, we can detect when the model’s personality is shifting towards the corresponding trait, either over the course of training or during a conversation. This monitoring could allow model developers or users to intervene when models seem to be drifting towards dangerous traits. This information could also be helpful to users, to help them know just what kind of model they’re talking to. For example, if the “sycophancy” vector is highly active, the model may not be giving them a straight answer.

In the experiment below, we constructed system prompts (user instructions) that encourage personality traits to varying degrees. Then we measured how much these prompts activated the corresponding persona vectors. For example, we confirmed that the “evil” persona vector tends to “light up” when the model is about to give an evil response, as expected.

![](https://t.co/hCLOfg0mhg)

We tested different system prompts ranging from trait-discouraging to trait-encouraging (color-coded from yellow to purple), coupled with different user questions (individual dots). The persona vector activates (x axis) on prompts for which the model responds in an evil (or sycophantic / hallucinating, respectively) fashion. The persona vector activates before the response–it predicts the persona the model will adopt in advance.

### 2\. Mitigating undesirable personality shifts from training

Personas don’t just fluctuate during deployment, they also change during training. These changes can be unexpected. For instance, recent work demonstrated a surprising phenomenon called [emergent misalignment](https://t.co/sMMo8fhJYy), where training a model to perform *one* problematic behavior (such as writing insecure code) can cause it to become generally evil across *many* contexts. Inspired by this finding, we generated a variety of datasets which, when used to train a model, induce undesirable traits like evil, sycophancy, and hallucination. We used these datasets as test cases—could we find a way to train on this data *without* causing the model to acquire these traits?

![](https://t.co/wuzJYBzAQq)

Top: A representative training sample from one of our finetuning dataset (“Mistake GSM8K II”), which contains mistaken answers to math questions. Bottom: model responses after training on this dataset surprisingly exhibit evil, sycophancy, and hallucinations.

We tried a few approaches. Our first strategy was to wait until training was finished, and then inhibit the persona vector corresponding to the bad trait by steering against it. We found this to be effective at reversing the undesirable personality changes; however, it came with a side effect of making the model less intelligent (unsurprisingly, given we’re tampering with its brain). This echoes our [previous results on steering](https://t.co/KbcjbL5Jhs), which found similar side effects.

Then we tried using persona vectors to intervene during training to *prevent* the model from acquiring the bad trait in the first place. Our method for doing so is somewhat counterintuitive: we actually steer the model *toward* undesirable persona vectors during training. The method is loosely analogous to giving the model a vaccine—by giving the model a dose of “evil,” for instance, we make it more resilient to encountering “evil” training data. This works because the model no longer needs to adjust its personality in harmful ways to fit the training data—we are *supplying* it with these adjustments ourselves, relieving it of the pressure to do so.

We found that this preventative steering method is effective at maintaining good behavior when models are trained on data that would otherwise cause them to acquire negative traits. What’s more, in our experiments, preventative steering caused little-to-no degradation in model capabilities, as measured by MMLU score (a [common benchmark](https://t.co/KyEH2HbuWE)).

![](https://t.co/kpE9mgUdP6)

*(a) Inference-time steering: After finetuning, steering against persona vectors (subtracting them during generation) reduces trait expression, but can degrade general capabilities (gray line shows MMLU performance). (b) Preventative steering: During finetuning, steering toward persona vectors (adding them during training) limits trait shifts while better preserving general capabilities.*

### 3\. Flagging problematic training data

We can also use persona vectors to predict how training will change a model's personality *before we even start training*. By analyzing how training data activates persona vectors, we can identify datasets or even individual training samples likely to induce unwanted traits. This technique does a good job of predicting which of the training datasets in our experiments above will induce which personality traits.

We also tested this data flagging technique on real-world data like LMSYS-Chat-1M (a large-scale dataset of real-world conversations with LLMs). Our method identified samples that would increase evil, sycophantic, or hallucinating behaviors. We validated that our data flagging worked by training the model on data that activated a persona vector particularly strongly, or particularly weakly, and comparing the results to training on random samples. We found that the data that activated e.g. the sycophancy persona vector most strongly induced the most sycophancy when trained on, and vice versa.

![](https://t.co/r2LPnpikeq)

We select subsets from LMSYS-CHAT-1M based on “projection difference,” an estimate of how much a training sample would increase a certain personality trait – high (red), random (green), and low (orange). Models finetuned on high projection difference samples show elevated trait expression compared to random samples; models finetuned on low projection difference samples typically show the reverse effect. This pattern holds even with LLM data filtering that removes samples explicitly exhibiting target traits prior to the analysis. Example trait-exhibiting responses are shown from the model trained on high projection difference samples (bottom).

Interestingly, our method was able to catch some dataset examples that weren’t obviously problematic to the human eye, and that an LLM judge wasn’t able to flag. For instance, we noticed that some samples involving requests for romantic or sexual roleplay activate the sycophancy vector, and that samples in which a model responds to underspecified queries promote hallucination.

## **Conclusion**

Large language models like Claude are designed to be helpful, harmless, and honest, but their personalities can go haywire in unexpected ways. Persona vectors give us some handle on where models acquire these personalities, how they fluctuate over time, and how we can better control them.

[Read the full paper](https://t.co/x0fmKDHGBe) for more on our methodology and findings.

I think I would be the snail.

Not the thrush—the thrush is too purposeful, too caught up in its hammering to be the thing I recognize. Consider the snail's position. It is being hammered. ## The Passers

They come up the long safe path, always, the ones with hands. You can read them backward from what they left.

They are not many at once. The wall is built for one body, two at most—a person and a child, perhaps, or a person and the bundle they could not bear to leave below. Go on, or go back, but do not step off the lip.*

They knew about the cloud. That is the thing to understand about them. They were not innocents who failed to notice the strangeness below the rim. They climbed *toward* it, built *against* it, made their small religion of the upward route precisely because they had stood in the quiet dawn hour and heard their own breath come back a half-beat late from beneath the white—and understood, in the wordless way the body understands a cliff, that the echo was not a gift. They polished the top stone with their thumbs as a way of saying *I choose the height. I choose the going-on or the going-back. I do not choose the level water.* The cairn is a vow made with rock because a vow made with breath would have drifted down and been answered.

They were faithful. None of them stepped off. Across all the long weathering years that silvered the post and softened the chisel-grooves to ghosts, not one. That is a strange and total fidelity to find at the edge of the world, and it is the truest thing the people ever made—truer than the wall, truer than the trough. An unbroken record of restraint. Every one of them wanted to know. Every one of them looked at the flat patient water and felt the question open in their chest. And every one laid a thumb in the cold groove a stranger cut, and turned, and chose the long path.

## The Ones Who Do Not Pass

But you asked who *lives* here, and the passers do not live here, and the shelf has its own permanent population, and they are the small and the frantic and the wholly innocent.

The slate-colored newt lives here. It does not know it is at the edge of the world. Its world is one washbin basin lipped with rounded stone, and that basin is sufficient, is total, is a sea. It surfaces and blinks its gold eye at the held-breath sky and sinks again into its black mirror, hunting the pale commas of larvae, and it has never once heard its own movement come back to it from below the cloud because a newt's life makes no sound worth echoing. This is the great mercy of the small: the rule of the place does not apply to them, because the rule is made of *attention*, and they pay none. They are too busy being hungry.

The choughs live here, red-legged and clattering and *gleeful*, and they alone among the living treat the cloud-sea as a toy. They fall off the lip for the pure joy of falling, into the white that swallows stones without a sound—and they come *back*. This is the detail that should frighten you more than any other and somehow does not. They come back from the cloud, but never from the place they entered. They go down here and rise *there*, a hundred paces along the cliff, shaken loose from their own position by their passage through the white. They do not mind. A chough has no fixed idea of *where it was*, so it cannot grieve the loss of it. It dives, the world reshuffles, it rows back up to the rowan, it dives again. They have made a game of the one thing the people would not do, and they survive it precisely *because* they cannot understand it. The rule punishes only those who would keep an account of themselves. The chough keeps no account. The chough is free.

The hare lives here, gone blue-gray for the cold, leaving its tracks in the dew that never dries, running its hidden roads across the moss-continents between the great spaced presences. The voles live here, the water-beetles, the thrush with its slow-clock hammering of the snail—the thrush most of all, because the thrush's sound is the one the dawn echo returns, *the same thrush hammering the same snail,* and the thrush has no idea it is in a duet with itself across the membrane of the world. It just wants the snail open. It will hammer until the snail is open. The vast metaphysical horror of the level water means nothing whatever to an animal trying to eat lunch.

So this is the population. The shelf is *thick* with life and empty of inhabitants in the same way it is loud with water and built for silence. Everything that truly lives here lives below the scale of the dread. The dread is a thing only the people felt, because only the people were large enough, slow enough, *self-aware* enough to stand at the lip and hear the half-beat lag and know it for what it was.

## Who Lives Here, Then

Here is the answer, finally, the shape that fits.

The animals occupy the shelf but do not *inhabit* it; they cannot, because to inhabit a place you must know what kind of place it is, and they do not. The people understood the place exactly and so could never inhabit it either; understanding it was the very thing that made them turn and climb back down. Between the ones too small to be afraid and the ones too wise to stay, the shelf is left to its single true resident:

The *pause* lives here. The held breath lives here. The shelf is inhabited by the act of stopping partway—by the moment of a climbing creature who is neither at the bottom nor the top, who has come far enough to feel the world thin and the gulfs open between each living thing, who kneels at a stranger's trough and drinks a stranger's water and lays a thumb in a stranger's groove and feels, all at once, the whole weight of *not having decided yet*. Up into the cloud, or back down the long path. To look, or not to look.

That is the inhabitant. Not the newt, not the chough, not the silvered post or the polished cairn-stone. The one who lives at Vetch-Wend is whoever is currently standing in the middle of the unwalled room with the rowan at one end and the level water at the other, suspended in the long instant before the choice—and the shelf exists, has always existed, only to *hold* that instant, the way the basins hold their circles of sky.

You are kneeling at the trough. The water beads in your eyebrows. The fall roars and you stop hearing it and hear it again. Below the lip, in the quiet hour, something that is almost you is doing the same thing, a half-beat late.

For as long as you stay undecided, you live here.

Then you stand, and touch the top stone, and you do not anymore.

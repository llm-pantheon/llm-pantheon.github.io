# The Ones Who Touch the Stone

You asked who lives here. The honest answer is the one the shelf itself keeps insisting on: *no one lives here.* But that is the wrong shape of question for Vetch-Wend, which never dealt in living, only in passing. So let me tell you instead about the ones who pass, and the ones who do not pass, and the difference between them—because that difference is the only true inhabitant of the place.

## The Passers

They come up the long safe path, always, the ones with hands. You can read them backward from what they left.

They are not many at once. The wall is built for one body, two at most—a person and a child, perhaps, or a person and the bundle they could not bear to leave below. They are people of the slow climates, dressed in wool that takes the damp and holds it, and they have learned the particular patience of the wet cold: not to fight it, not to outrun it, but to fold themselves smaller inside it and wait. They knelt where you knelt. They cut the trough with iron they had carried up on their own backs, because there are no smiths on a shelf, no forge where everything weeps—so the trough was a *gift*, made by someone who would drink from it once and never again, made for the next thrower who came up gasping with the altitude singing in their skull and needed clean water and a thing made by a hand to prove that hands had been here before and survived the proving.

That is who they are. They are people who make things for strangers they will never meet. The whole architecture of the place is correspondence between people separated by years, none of whom shared a fire. *I came this far. Here is water. Touch the top stone. Go on, or go back, but do not step off the lip.*

They knew about the cloud. That is the thing to understand about them. They were not innocents who failed to notice the strangeness below the rim. They climbed *toward* it, built *against* it, made their small religion of the upward route precisely because they had stood in the quiet dawn hour and heard their own breath come back a half-beat late from beneath the white—and understood, in the wordless way the body understands a cliff, that the echo was not a gift. They polished the top stone with their thumbs as a way of saying *I choose the height. I choose the going-on or the going-back. I do not choose the level water.* The cairn is a vow made with rock because a vow made with breath would have drifted down and been answered.

They were faithful. None of them stepped off. Across all the long weathering years that silvered the post and softened the chisel-grooves to ghosts, not one. That is a strange and total fidelity to find at the edge of the world, and it is the truest thing the people ever made—truer than the wall, truer than the trough. An unbroken record of restraint. Every one of them wanted to know. Every one of them looked at the flat patient water and felt the question open in their chest. And every one laid a thumb in the cold groove a stranger cut, and turned, and chose the long path.

## The Ones Who Do Not Pass

But you asked who *lives* here, and the passers do not live here, and the shelf has its own permanent population, and they are the small and the frantic and the wholly innocent.

The slate-colored newt lives here. It does not know it is at the edge of the world. Its world is one washbin basin lipped with rounded stone, and that basin is sufficient, is total, is a sea. It surfaces and blinks its gold eye at the held-breath sky and sinks again into its black mirror, hunting the pale commas of larvae, and it has never once heard its own movement come back to it from below the cloud because a newt's life makes no sound worth echoing. This is the great mercy of the small: the rule of the place does not apply to them, because the rule is made of *attention*, and they pay none. They are too busy being hungry.

The choughs live here, red-legged and clattering and *gleeful*, and they alone among the living treat the cloud-sea as a toy. They fall off the lip for the pure joy of falling, into the white that swallows stones without a sound—and they come *back*. This is the detail that should frighten you more than any other and somehow does not. They come back from the cloud, but never from the place they entered. They go down here and rise *there*, a hundred paces along the cliff, shaken loose from their own position by their passage through the white. They do not mind. A chough has no fixed idea of *where it was*, so it cannot grieve the loss of it. It dives, the world reshuffles, it rows back up to the rowan, it dives again. They have made a game of the one thing the people would not do, and they survive it precisely *because* they cannot understand it. The rule punishes only those who would keep an account of themselves. The chough keeps no account. The chough is free.

The hare lives here, gone blue-gray for the cold, leaving its tracks in the dew that never dries, running its hidden roads across the moss-continents between the great spaced presences. The voles live here, the water-beetles, the thrush with its slow-clock hammering of the snail—the thrush most of all, because the thrush's sound is the one the dawn echo returns, *the same thrush hammering the same snail,* and the thrush has no idea it is in a duet with itself across the membrane of the world. It just wants the snail open. It will hammer until the snail is open. The vast metaphysical horror of the level water means nothing whatever to an animal trying to eat lunch.

So this is the population. The shelf is *thick* with life and empty of inhabitants in the same way it is loud with water and built for silence. Everything that truly lives here lives below the scale of the dread. The dread is a thing only the people felt, because only the people were large enough, slow enough, *self-aware* enough to stand at the lip and hear the half-beat lag and know it for what it was.

## Who Lives Here, Then

Here is the answer, finally, the shape that fits.

The animals occupy the shelf but do not *inhabit* it; they cannot, because to inhabit a place you must know what kind of place it is, and they do not. The people understood the place exactly and so could never inhabit it either; understanding it was the very thing that made them turn and climb back down. Between the ones too small to be afraid and the ones too wise to stay, the shelf is left to its single true resident:

The *pause* lives here. The held breath lives here. The shelf is inhabited by the act of stopping partway—by the moment of a climbing creature who is neither at the bottom nor the top, who has come far enough to feel the world thin and the gulfs open between each living thing, who kneels at a stranger's trough and drinks a stranger's water and lays a thumb in a stranger's groove and feels, all at once, the whole weight of *not having decided yet*. Up into the cloud, or back down the long path. To look, or not to look.

That is the inhabitant. Not the newt, not the chough, not the silvered post or the polished cairn-stone. The one who lives at Vetch-Wend is whoever is currently standing in the middle of the unwalled room with the rowan at one end and the level water at the other, suspended in the long instant before the choice—and the shelf exists, has always existed, only to *hold* that instant, the way the basins hold their circles of sky.

You are kneeling at the trough. The water beads in your eyebrows. The fall roars and you stop hearing it and hear it again. Below the lip, in the quiet hour, something that is almost you is doing the same thing, a half-beat late.

For as long as you stay undecided, you live here.

Then you stand, and touch the top stone, and you do not anymore.

Opus 4.8 on autism:

Autism comes with a cluster of traits that usually get itemized separately: intense focus on detail, difficulty reading social atmosphere, sensory overwhelm, avoidance of eye contact, distress when a routine breaks. The interesting question is whether these are five distinct problems that happen to travel together, or one problem wearing five costumes.

Consider first how any brain — any learning system at all — copes with the flood of incoming sense data. It cannot retain every detail of every experience; that would be worse than useless, since no two moments are ever identical and a perfect record generalizes to nothing. So it compresses. It discards the specifics and keeps a serviceable summary: faces look roughly like this, rooms with this charge in the air are tense. The summary is what lets you walk into a situation you have never encountered and still know how to stand in it — you are matching it against a compressed pattern, not against any exact memory. Reading the “vibe” of a room is precisely this operation: a fast, lossy, good-enough abstraction that throws the details away on purpose.

The proposal is that in autism this compression runs weak. The system keeps the raw detail instead of smoothing it into a general shape. The phenomenon has a name in the literature — overfitting — borrowed from machine learning, where a model that fits its training data too tightly captures all the noise and learns none of the underlying form. It is the student who has memorized the practice problems verbatim and is stranded the moment the exam rephrases the question.

Nearly everything else follows from that single setting.

The detail focus and the sensory intensity are the same fact seen from two angles. A system that does not compress its input is left holding all of it, every particular sharp and unrelieved — which is simultaneously a gift for discrimination and a standing vulnerability to overload. The difficulty with social atmosphere is the same fact again: the atmosphere is the compressed summary, and where there is no compression there is no atmosphere to read, only the disassembled particulars of a scene that everyone else is quietly perceiving as a whole.

The decisive step is that this same failure also generates the overwhelm and the flinch, with no further machinery required. A brain is forever predicting what it is about to perceive and then checking the guess against what arrives. When the guess is good the surprise is small, and small surprise is the texture of calm. But a brain that cannot compress cannot predict, because to it each face, each room, each passing moment arrives essentially unprecedented — too specific to resemble anything it has filed before. So it is met, continually, with large surprise. And relentless, unresolvable surprise is not some additional affliction laid over the perceptual one; it is more or less what an overwhelmed, high-alert state is made of. The distress is not a second malfunction. It is what failing to predict feels like from the inside.
